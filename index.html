Methodology & Financial Glossary

This tool bridges the gap between daily restaurant operations and institutional private equity logic. When evaluating the viability of a concept or approaching a bank for funding, understanding *why* these numbers interact is as important as the numbers themselves. Here is a clear explanation of the forces at play.

Prime Costs (The Operator's Pulse)

What it is: Cost of Goods Sold (Food & Paper) + Total Labor (including payroll taxes and benefits).

Why it matters: This is the most controllable expense in a restaurant. A healthy restaurant **must keep Prime Costs below 60%** of gross sales. If your proforma shows Prime Costs at 65%+, the concept is fundamentally flawed and will likely fail to generate enough cash to cover fixed rent and debt.

EBITDA vs. Free Cash Flow

What it is: EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) shows the raw operating profit of the concept. Free Cash Flow (FCF) is what is actually left in your bank account.

Why it matters: Beginners confuse Net Income or EBITDA with cash in their pocket. This tool calculates FCF by taking EBITDA, paying the taxes, and—crucially—subtracting the cash used to pay down the principal balance of your bank loan.

DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)

What it is: Your EBITDA divided by your total annual debt payments (Interest + Principal).

Why it matters: This is the exact metric a bank underwriter uses to approve your loan. A DSCR of 1.0x means you make exactly enough to pay the bank. Banks generally require a **minimum DSCR of 1.25x** to grant a loan, proving you have a 25% buffer if sales drop.

Cash-on-Cash Return & Payback

What it is: Free Cash Flow divided by the initial Equity (Cash) you put into the deal.

Why it matters: Private equity firms don't look at total profit; they look at capital efficiency. If you invest $200k in cash and generate $50k in FCF, your Cash-on-Cash return is 25%. The payback period calculates the exact decimal year your cumulative FCF turns positive (meaning you recouped your initial investment).

Inflation & Compounding Growth

What it is: Variable costs (COGS, CC fees) scale immediately with sales volume. But fixed OPEX (Rent, Utilities, Admin) generally inflate with time, regardless of how busy you are.

Why it matters: The tool allows you to adjust "Fixed OPEX Inflation". If Labor grows at 3% a year but Sales only grow at 1%, your EBITDA margin will collapse by Year 3. This exposes the "silent killers" of long-term restaurant viability.

Credit Card Capture

What it is: The percentage of your gross sales paid via credit cards rather than cash.

Why it matters: In today's landscape, this is typically 90%–98%. Because credit card processors charge ~2.5% to 3.0%, this represents a massive operational expense. You can now adjust this capture rate in the inputs to see how heavily merchant fees eat into your bottom line.
